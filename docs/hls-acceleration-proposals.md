# HLS Acceleration Proposals

**Date:** October 29–30, 2025
**Context:** Response to Acting Administrator Duffy's October 20 recompete directive

## SpaceX Proposal: Simplified Starship Architecture

SpaceX proposed a **stripped-down Starship HLS** mission architecture emphasizing
speed and crew safety over full cargo capacity:

- **Expendable refueling tankers**: Eliminating airfoils, heat shields, and landing
  hardware on tanker variants to reduce mass and simplify production
- **Low lunar orbit rendezvous**: Abandoning the NRHO (Near-Rectilinear Halo Orbit)
  rendezvous in favor of low lunar orbit, which has lower energy requirements and
  is safer for mission abort scenarios
- **Reduced refueling flights**: The simplified architecture would require fewer
  tanker flights than the original plan

SpaceX defended Starship HLS as the only vehicle with the cargo capacity to support
sustained lunar surface operations, arguing that short-term simplifications could
achieve the first landing while preserving long-term capability.

## Blue Origin Proposal: Blue Moon MK1 Path

Blue Origin proposed using the **Blue Moon MK1** — a simpler, uncrewed lander variant
that differs from the larger MK2 under their existing Artemis V contract:

- **No orbital refueling required**: MK1 uses a direct-to-lunar-surface architecture,
  eliminating the most technically challenging aspect of the Starship HLS plan
- **First MK1 unit already assembled**: As of October 2025, Blue Origin reported
  the first MK1 hardware was fully assembled
- **Uncrewed cargo delivery first**: Proposed an uncrewed MK1 demonstration mission
  potentially as early as 2026
- **Multiple MK1 missions**: Could deliver crew and cargo to the lunar surface using
  multiple smaller landers rather than one large vehicle

## Evaluation

NASA confirmed both proposals were received and that a committee of subject matter
experts would evaluate acceleration options. The evaluation fed into the broader
Artemis architecture review that incoming Administrator Isaacman would complete
in early 2026.

## Broader Industry Interest

- **Lockheed Martin** announced it would assemble an industry team to respond to
  NASA's forthcoming Request for Information
- Multiple smaller companies expressed interest in the open competition

## Outcome

These proposals directly informed Isaacman's February 27, 2026 "course correction":
- Artemis III redesigned as an LEO systems test (not a landing)
- Artemis IV designated as the first crewed landing (2028 target)
- Both SpaceX and Blue Origin remain in competition: "The first company that is
  capable of delivering a lander to take American astronauts to the lunar surface
  and back is the one that this nation is going to go with." — Isaacman
