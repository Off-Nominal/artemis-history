# HLS Lander Competition Reopened

**Date:** October 20, 2025
**Announced by:** Acting Administrator Sean Duffy

## Decision

Acting NASA Administrator Sean Duffy announces that NASA will **open up the
Artemis III lunar lander contract to competition**, ending SpaceX's position as
the sole Artemis III HLS provider.

## Rationale

1. **SpaceX Starship HLS behind schedule**: Starship had not yet achieved a
   successful orbital flight with full recovery. The critical on-orbit propellant
   transfer technology required for lunar missions remained unproven after
   eight integrated flight tests.
2. **Race with China**: China's stated goal of landing taikonauts on the Moon by
   2030 created urgency. President Trump wanted Americans on the Moon before
   leaving office in January 2029.
3. **Simpler alternatives exist**: Blue Origin's Blue Moon MK1, a smaller uncrewed
   lander, does not require orbital refueling and was further along in assembly.

## Key Quotes (Duffy)

- "They're behind. They've pushed their timelines out, and we're in a race
  against China."
- "We're not going to wait for one company. We're going to push this forward
  and win the second space race."
- "The president and I want to get to the moon in this president's term, so
  I'm gonna open up the contract."

## Actions Taken

- Directed SpaceX and Blue Origin to submit acceleration proposals by October 29
- Planned to issue a Request for Information (RFI) to the broader aerospace industry
- Assembled a committee of NASA subject matter experts to evaluate proposals
- Highlighted Blue Origin's Blue Moon MK1 as a potential faster path

## Industry Response

- **Elon Musk** attacked Duffy on X/Twitter (Oct 21), calling him "Sean Dummy"
  and questioning his qualifications
- **Former NASA Administrators** Bridenstine and Bolden supported the recompete
  at the von Braun Symposium
- **Lockheed Martin** announced plans to convene an industry team to respond to
  NASA's RFI, noting ongoing technical analysis for human lunar landers

## Impact on Artemis Architecture

This decision set in motion the chain of events that led to Isaacman's February 2026
"course correction," which restructured Artemis III from a lunar landing into an
LEO systems test and pushed the first landing to Artemis IV.
