# Crew on EM-1 Feasibility Study

## Background

On February 15, 2017, Acting NASA Administrator Robert Lightfoot announced
that NASA would study the feasibility of adding crew to Exploration Mission 1
(EM-1), which had been planned as an uncrewed SLS/Orion test flight.

The request originated from the Trump transition team, with NASA White House
Liaison Greg Autry as a key advocate. The concept was compared to an "Apollo 8
moment" — a bold decision to fly crew earlier than planned.

## Lightfoot Memo (February 15, 2017)

Lightfoot directed Associate Administrator Bill Gerstenmaier to "initiate a
study to assess the feasibility of adding a crew to Exploration Mission-1"
and to "examine the opportunities it could present to accelerate the effort
of the first crewed flight."

The announcement was made at the SLS/Orion Suppliers Conference in Washington
and communicated to all NASA employees via agency-wide memo.

## Rationale

- Demonstrate tangible human spaceflight progress early in the new administration
- Accelerate crewed deep-space flight (EM-2 was projected for 2021–2023)
- Avoid Constellation's fate of being canceled before flying crew
- Geopolitical competition — reassert U.S. leadership in deep space

## Technical Concerns

- **Life support (ECLSS)**: Not planned for integration until EM-2. Adding it
  would have required implementing a full ECLSS three years ahead of schedule.
- **Launch Abort System**: Two additional qualification tests still required
  before crew certification
- **Crew systems**: Seats, displays, controls, and crew interfaces not yet developed
- **Safety panels**: The Aerospace Safety Advisory Panel and Astronaut Office had
  consistently opposed crewing the first SLS flight
- **Mission change**: The crewed version would carry two astronauts on an 8–9 day
  mission, with one day in high Earth orbit to verify life support before the
  Trans-Lunar Injection burn

## Decision (May 12, 2017)

Acting Administrator Lightfoot announced that EM-1 would remain uncrewed:

> "After evaluating cost, risk, and technical factors in a project of this
> magnitude, it is difficult to accommodate changes needed for a crewed EM-1
> mission at this time."

The study found adding crew was technically feasible but would cost an
additional **$600–900 million** and push the mission well into the 2020s.
NASA cited **cost — not safety — as the primary reason** for the decision.

## Key Players

- **Robert Lightfoot** — Acting Administrator, ordered the study
- **Bill Gerstenmaier** — Associate Administrator for HEOMD, conducted the study
- **Greg Autry** — Trump transition team / White House Liaison, key political advocate
- **Aerospace Safety Advisory Panel** — Opposed crewing the first flight
