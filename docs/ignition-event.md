# NASA Ignition Event

**Date:** March 24, 2026
**Announced by:** NASA Administrator Jared Isaacman

NASA's "Ignition" event unveiled sweeping agencywide initiatives to implement the
2025 National Space Policy and accelerate American leadership in space. The event
featured three livestreamed sessions beginning at 9 a.m. ET.

## Artemis Program Updates

### Mission Cadence Acceleration
- After Artemis IV and V, NASA will use commercially procured, reusable hardware
  for frequent crewed lunar landings
- Initial target: one landing every six months, with potential to increase
- Added a standardized SLS configuration flight in 2027
- Artemis III (2027) confirmed as an Earth-orbit integrated systems test

### Lunar Base (Three-Phase Plan)
NASA announced a permanent lunar base near the south pole by ~2030, with
approximately $20 billion allocated over seven years.

**Phase 1 — "Build, Test, Learn":** Accelerated robotic missions via CLPS,
sending rovers, instruments, hoppers, and drones to test mobility, power,
communications, and science.

**Phase 2 — "Establish Early Infrastructure":** Semi-habitable facilities with
recurring astronaut operations. Includes JAXA's pressurized rover and
international partner hardware.

**Phase 3 — "Enable Long-Duration Presence":** Heavier permanent infrastructure
including ASI (Italian Space Agency) Multi-Purpose Habitats and CSA Lunar
Utility Vehicle.

### CLPS 2.0
New competitive follow-on Commercial Lunar Payload Services program:
- 10-year ordering period, 15-year execution timeline
- Capped at $6 billion
- Targets up to 30 robotic landings starting in 2027

### Lunar Terrain Vehicle Services
Multiple crewed and uncrewed rovers with initial operating capability by 2028.

### Lunar Gateway Paused
The Lunar Gateway has been paused indefinitely (not formally canceled). Some
Gateway hardware is being reallocated to the Space Reactor-1 Freedom nuclear
Mars mission.

## Space Reactor-1 Freedom (Mars Mission)

NASA will launch SR-1 Freedom, the first nuclear-powered interplanetary
spacecraft, to Mars before the end of 2028 (targeting December 2028).

- **Propulsion**: Nuclear electric propulsion using ~20-kilowatt fission reactor
  with uranium oxide fuel; reactor activates within 48 hours of launch
- **Transit**: Approximately one year to Mars
- **Payload**: Three Ingenuity-class "Skyfall" helicopters that deploy mid-air
  during atmospheric entry and land autonomously (no sky-crane)
- **Instruments**: Ground-penetrating radar to map subsurface water ice and
  scout future human landing sites
- **Heritage**: Uses reallocated Lunar Gateway hardware
- **Significance**: First-ever use of nuclear fission propulsion on an
  interplanetary mission

A Mars Telecom Network was also announced to support future Mars operations.

## LEO Transition Strategy

NASA acknowledged no commercial space stations are ready to replace ISS and
announced a phased transition:

- **ISS extension**: Operations extended through the mid-2030s
- **Government Core Module**: NASA will procure a new U.S. Core Module that
  initially attaches to ISS for validation
- **Commercial modules**: Additional commercial modules attach to ISS for
  validation, then detach into free flight
- **Post-ISS**: Core Module becomes foundation of hybrid LEO ecosystem
- **Demand**: Two crew missions and four to five cargo missions annually to
  future commercial LEO destinations

## Science Missions

- **Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope**: Launching fall 2026, ahead of schedule
  and under budget
- **Dragonfly**: Nuclear-powered octocopter to Titan, launching 2028, arriving 2034
- **ESA Rosalind Franklin Rover**: Launching to Mars 2028 with NASA's MOMA
  instrument; NASA now administering the mission
- **NEO Surveyor**: Targeting 2027 launch

## Workforce

NASA announced plans to convert thousands of contractor positions to civil
service and restore engineering and technical competencies in-house.
